An Interface to Help NICU Nurses Save Babies in Uganda

Every year 3 million babies die in the first month of life. Most deaths occur in the developing world and countries like Uganda, where Infant wards (NICU) are severely understaffed.

An incredible startup based in Chicago has set out to do something about it. Teresa Cauvel and Sona Shah started Neopenda because they have a shared passion for global health. When they met at Columbia, the pair developed a design solution using their engineering and research backgrounds. They created a 4-in-1 vital sign monitor that tracks infant pulse, respiration, temperature, and blood oxygen levels, with one inexpensive, lightweight device.

The Challenge

Our challenge was to create an attractive, functional, android tablet interface built on the existing UX prototype being tested in Uganda. Four UI designers and I collaborated on a 4-week process design and test a solution focussed on understanding the user.

The design would need to consider cultural roadblocks and design around them. Common assumptions based on familiar cultural and emotional associations weren’t going to be available in the design process. Since none of us were either SMEs or Africans, we had to lean heavily on research.

As we read through the domain research materials, we categorized project goals by user type—because we immediately noticed some competing interests/needs.

01
Stakeholders

Want a sleek, attractive product they can sell to hospitals of all sizes.

02
Ugandan Nurses

Will prioritize ease of use, and want a maximum amount of control without tons of steps.

03
Doctors/Admin

Want visibility into nurse’s activities to make decisions about resources, staffing, and structure.

we decided to focus attention on the nurse’s goals, as they’re the ones with the ability to save the most lives.
Design Principles

After and honing in on our target nurse, and synthesizing more research my team and I came up with the following design principles to guide our process, and justify design decisions:

Invert the Pyramid

The design should synthesize data prior to nurses picking up the tablet interface. The most important info should be at the top of the screen and prominently displayed.

Birds Eye View

Both doctors and nurses need to put the specific information they’re seeing into a broader context in order to spot patterns that the platform can’t see.

Don’t Reinvent the Wheel

Ease is everything. Familiar patterns, readable type and intuitive buttons are a must. The design should fit into a nurse’s life without extensive training or undue complexity.

Meet Abbo: The NICU Nurse
Evaluating UX Axure Prototype Against Abbo’s Needs

Let’s imagine a 40 year old NICU nurse named Abbo. Abbo works extremely long hours at a NICU in Uganda and volunteers a lot of her time because of limited resources in her community. Her hospital only has the budget to hire her and one part-time junior assistant. They’re in charge of watching over 20 babies, and have to provide life saving care around the clock. Abbo also struggles with alarm fatigue because on certain days there’s at least one alarm sounding for hours on end.

The current functionality helps Abbo in 3 ways:
  • When a baby’s vital exits a normal range, the system needs to alarm Abbo, and that alarm should be visible in more than one modality.
  • The device has a “locate” light triggered by the app that will flash when Abbo touches that button on the interface to make it easier to find babies in the NICU.
  • Doctors and nurses like Abbo can see this information in temporal context. A “trends” section that helps her see how a vital had changed over time.
Opportunities to Improve

My team and I had to carefully consider both the limitations of a four- week timeframe and the feasibility of correctly identifying problems we deemed most pertinent to Ugandan nurses like Abbo.

Alarm Fatigue

We know that Abbo’s hospital has an understaffing problem - so we theorized the dashboard could be doing more to give Abbo a feeling of full control/visibility without overwhelming her. The current prototype wasn’t making it clear what she should focus on.

Triage Alarms

The current dashboard wasn’t doing enough to help nurses triage the alarms in high stress situations. The dashboard should be offering Abbo some indication of which problem or alarming vital was most important, and display information in accordance with a defined hierarchy.

Analysis of other Vital Sign Monitors

To understand how best to assist Abbo in the NICU, the next step for us was to do a competitive analysis. We researched 5 prominent devices and I was struck by two commonalities among these competitor UIs, both of which offered additional opportunities to improve:

01
Dated

None looked particularly modern or sleek. We understood this wasn’t an indication Abbo longed for modernity; instead an indication of her priorities: accuracy, customization, and readability.

02
Color Only

In many instances, color was the only visual indication for Abbo (aside from the number itself) that a baby’s vital reading was critical.

Exploring Solutions
Creating Style Tiles that Build Visual Frameworks for Abbo

Our visual explorations each focussed on prioritizing information in the interface, and visual patterns that reinforce the new hierarchy. We understood that Abbo’s preference for what’s most important will be situational, and I came up with divergent concepts that reflect that variability:

01

Tile Rankings

This concept will help Abbo decide which of her patient’s vital is most pressing, and explores physical rearrangement of inner readings. Enabling Abbo to read critical babies visually across a vertical column will shorten her workflow and help her build quick mental action plans.

Secondly, thinking about the nurse's desire for control,the tags were a way to put extra sorting power into their hands.

02

Whitespace and Pulsing

This concept will help Abbo with her headache 9 hours into an 11 hour shift. To solve for excessive visual noise I explored reducing the number of visual lines in the interface to make it easier to process. Using pulses instead of flashing in this concept will also help Abbo with her alarm fatigue. By exploring a softer alarm trigger that still got her attention perhaps she’d become more effective without feeling fatigued.

03

Mapping

This concept considers the mental strain Abbo has remembering where babies are located in the NICU. By arranging babies on screen the same way they’re arranged in the NICU she’ll find patients quicker. The urgency meter below the map is one exploration of a triage system I developed to help Abbo make smarter judgment calls as alarms sound simultaneously.

Testing our Concepts
Learning from NICU nurses which elements were resonating

As a team, we had a total of 12 directions to test with a group of SMEs given to us by Teresa and Sona. Devising a system to reduce bias and get the best possible information was a bit tricky.

I discovered a UX technique called “Triading”, in which users are asked to rank order a large set of choices by comparing just 3 items at a time instead of considering the entire set at once. The benefits of using this method are that users can more easily share insights and gut reactions with a smaller cognitive load.

In each group, we asked users to pick the favorite and least favorite and explain why - referring to how usable, readable, or logical each layout/schema was. The interviews revealed a couple of interesting takeaways:

Users Craved Color

Users revealed that the lack of color in medical apps was frustrating and perhaps a cultural preference for bright colors in Uganda presented an opportunity.

The Challenge
An Employee Incentive Company in Need of a Stronger Visual Identity

Job hopping, remote work, and changes to the global workforce have left companies playing catch-up, and all asking themselves the same question:

How do we keep our employees happy, motivated, and fulfilled at work? Perks Worldwide is a B2B Employee and Channel incentives company that creates custom software to help companies keep their best from jumping ship. You can read about their mission and current product offerings here.

The marketing team was looking for creative ways to leverage their large content library and visual brand to generate inbound leads and maintain a healthy sales funnel. It’s important to Perks that the visual brand identity for this content unique, energetic, and distinct - particularly as a smaller player in the space.

Initial Research
Finding a fit between trendy and unique.

The primary users for the first set of rebranding materials were salespeople and C-level executives, so the designs had to tow a delicate balance between whimsy & professionalism. In collaboration with Creative Director, Christopher Spann, we worked towards improving the way the brand visually represented people in its content marketing materials.

Rigid Geometry

Most companies in the space were using characters defined by rigid geometry

Tiny Heads

6 of the top competitors in the space used characters with very small heads - yielding a clear opportunity for differentiation.

Emotionless

We noticed a lack of emotion among companies attempting to use their characters to discuss motivation

We both felt there was an opportunity to differentiate with a character design that based itself loosely on simple circular geometric elements - but had a flexible design system allowing for emotional and personality differentiation.
The Perkie Design System
Trends and Opportunities to differentiate from the competition

Given our research and understanding of the space we developed a design framework that felt unique, but carried some elements of traditional flat vector art. Our Design system would allow artwork to be created quickly, across multiple mediums, and by multiple designers.

The design system Chris and I developed was characterized by 5 major points of style:

no outlines.

but why?
In order for our “Perkies” to be created quickly, and in the maximum number of contexts, we decided to refrain from using outlines in our designs. Our characters would use the edges of the vector shapes themselves to differentiate space - placing a greater emphasis on color contrast.

eyes & legs.

what about them?
To ensure different characters were recognizable as part of the same branded set we came up with two rigid elements to always be used for new illustrations: eyes and legs that would be the same on every character, no matter what.

body shape.

yeah, what of it?
To give each character a similar feel, each “Perkie” always began from the same oval shaped torso elements. Clothing, poses, and other variances still adhered loosely to the oval torso shape.

head size.

go on...
Perkies, unlike vector art from other players in the space will always use oversized heads with widths roughly 1.5x wider than the shoulder to shoulder width. This made our characters feel unique, and made illustrating emotions easier.

no gradients.

why not?
To ensure consistency across web, print, and beyond Perkies should never use gradients or overtly three-dimensional elements; Perkies are designed to be flat and aren’t intended for super hi-fi executions.

no true blacks.

why not?
Perkies should always come second to the content - and when placed next to type shouldn’t use true blacks - and use dark greys in their place. Perkie character designs should grab the user’s attention on their own and not rely on excessive contrast ratios.

Reflections
Making the business case for illustration

The branding work my director and I did forced us to think systematically about custom artwork and create a process to fit illustration into the business' needs. We made decisions about when and where to use our character designs sparingly and I learned through trial and error how to sell these concepts to stakeholders. The final design system I developed was a compromise between what the business could feasibly support and what the creative team originally envisioned.

This project required me to study how best to implement illustration, and challenge assumptions other me made about the b2b industry. I was able to synthesize stakeholder feedback and deliver a usable system in use to this day.
